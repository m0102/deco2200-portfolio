Project: AuTour

A tour planning app for use in an autonomous tour bus to help tourists create a personalised schedule and unique experience while travelling.

Introduction

The Brief

‘Map out the interactions that will be required to assist people in interacting with autonomous vehicles in future cities.' More specifically: How do we create a unique and seamless experience for tourists using an autonomous tour bus?

The Solution

Our solution was to take away the rigidity of pre-planned tour scheduling, allowing users to visit only the places they want, in the order they choose. Users can browse and input their preferences for destinations, length of the tour and other activities. The app includes notifications based the autonomous vehicles’ data collection of traffic and crowding, which allows tourists to reroute the journey to avoid these in advance. Complete with additional features including a map, upcoming stops, choice of travel mode and the ability to edit the plan at any point on their journey.

The AuTour tour planning app is a seamless addition to a future autonomous bus which gives users complete control of their experience.

The Solution

Welcome:
A welcome screen prompting users to start their journey.
Categories:
A screen showing all the pages of the app with icons (also included in footer for easy navigation). Users can start with ‘Browse’ if they want to see all the possibilities or jump to ‘Plan Trip’ if they already know where they want to go. Once they have created a plan, they can go to ‘Edit plan’ to change it at any time. 'Dashboard' is the main screen they can use to see where they are, quickly change the route or see their next stops.
Browse - Browse All:
The ‘Browse’ pages have multiple categories, such as popular and fun activities, which users can scroll through for more specific lists, or ‘browse all’ to see an overview. The ‘browse all’ page has cards for each place/activity which users can add to their plan directly or tap for more info.
Browse - Popular Landmarks:
The ‘Popular Landmarks’ screen has information about each place related to the current journey, such as how long it will take to get there and how busy it is currently. Users can see more info or add it to their plan.
Plan Trip:
The ‘Plan Trip’ page has key categories for users to personalise and chose, including destinations, activities, and times. The bus then calculates a schedule based on this. They can quickly go back to the browse page to add places from there as well.
Day/Edit Plan:
The user’s 'Day Plan' is shown with icons and times. They can reorder, remove or add to this. The bus also provides suggestions to add based on their choices and current plan. Once they’re done they are directed to the 'Dashboard'.
Dashboard:
The ‘Dashboard’ page is a home page for the app, providing a map of where the user is, upcoming stops and options to change these. As well as notifications about traffic and overcrowding which they can respond to by skipping or rerouting the stop if they like.

Process & Decisions

We began the project by brainstorming different context areas and types of autonomous vehicles. Once we had decided on buses and the tourism context, we did primary research, including interviews with our key stakeholders; tourists and travellers, as well as observations of current tour buses in Sydney. We also did secondary research into tourism, tour buses and future autonomous buses.

We progressed to initial concepts and sketches. We focused on three key ideas; a planning app, browsing system and a booking/alert concept. We tested these with users using paper prototypes. From our findings, we iterated the planning app, choosing to combine the most successful elements from each idea into one. This iteration included a browsing section, a planning system and an alert for upcoming stops and issues with the route (e.g. Traffic). We tested this with users with the Pop app.

The feedback from this testing was used in the wireframes, in which we focused on element placement and simplifying the interface for users. We tested this, finding that that users desired a main page to return to and easier navigation. This led to the inclusion of the ‘Dashboard’ and footer.

Next, we created the final interface screens using continual testing to iterate and improve the idea from wireframes to final design. We changed the layout, colour and UI elements significantly during this process. We did a last round of testing and created the interactive prototype with Invision. This was well received by users who liked the design and found it easy to use and navigate.

Reflection

Overall, the project was very interesting to work on. It challenged our thinking about the future of transport and how we might interact with these systems. While autonomous vehicles are not fully developed or safe for all environments, this future is approaching and apps like AuTour will become crucial to develop for users. Throughout the project while certain difficulties arose, these were overcome through productive collaboration, resulting in a successful prototype which addresses the brief and our key user needs.
